# CORTEX_WORDING_BRIEF.md

## Goal

Help Cortex rewrite the VSF site so that visitors who **do not know what vetiver is** can quickly understand:
- what vetiver is
- why it matters
- what problems it can solve
- why VSF exists
- how to engage (donate, partner, propose a project, subscribe)

This is not only a brand-copy exercise. It is a **public education + donor conversion** exercise.

## Core Problem With Current Site Language

The migrated site still reads too much like:
- an inherited nonprofit website
- a generic environmental organization
- internal language written by people who already know vetiver

For a cold visitor, this creates a clarity problem:
- they do not know what vetiver is
- they do not know why they should care
- they do not know what makes it remarkable
- they do not know what VSF actually does with it

## Strategic Reframe

The site should be organized around a simple progression:

1. **What is vetiver?**
2. **Why is it remarkable?**
3. **What can it do for communities and landscapes?**
4. **How does VSF help?**
5. **How can I support or join this work?**

VSF should be presented as the organization that helps communities unlock the practical possibilities of vetiver.

## Messaging Principle

The site should not start by talking about VSF.
It should start by making the visitor curious about **vetiver** and its possibilities.

Bad order:
- who we are
- our mission
- our values
- donate

Better order:
- here is a remarkable plant
- here are the problems it can help solve
- here is what becomes possible when communities learn to use it
- here is how VSF helps make that happen
- here is how you can support that work

## Main Messaging Pillars

### Pillar 1 — Vetiver is practical
Avoid mystical or overly technical framing.
Frame vetiver as a practical plant-based tool.

Good language:
- a powerful plant for soil and water protection
- a low-cost tool for regeneration
- a practical solution for erosion, runoff, slope stabilization, and degraded land
- a community-scale technology

Avoid:
- overusing unexplained words like phytoremediation, bioengineering, ecosystem restoration without examples
- sounding academic too early

### Pillar 2 — Vetiver unlocks many possibilities
The site should make the visitor feel that vetiver is more versatile than expected.

Possibility language should include:
- stopping erosion
- slowing runoff
- stabilizing slopes
- protecting roads and infrastructure
- restoring degraded land
- helping farmers retain soil and moisture
- reducing dependence on costly agricultural inputs
- creating local economic opportunities from vetiver products

### Pillar 3 — VSF helps communities turn knowledge into action
VSF should not sound like a distant charity.
It should sound like an enabling organization.

Good framing:
- VSF helps communities learn, test, adapt, and scale vetiver-based solutions
- VSF supports education, technical guidance, pilots, partnerships, evidence, and visibility
- VSF helps translate vetiver knowledge into real projects

### Pillar 4 — Evidence matters
The site should not rely only on hope and values.
It should combine inspiration with evidence.

Use:
- field metrics
- before/after logic
- project stories
- transparent claims
- project-specific evidence pages or sections

### Pillar 5 — Every page should lead to a next action
Education without action is not enough.
Every major page should gently lead somewhere:
- donate
- support a project
- partner with VSF
- propose a project
- subscribe for updates

## Recommended Site Wording Structure

### Home Page
Goal: make people care about vetiver in under 10 seconds.

Recommended structure:
1. Hero: introduce vetiver as a remarkable practical solution
2. “What is vetiver?” section
3. “What can it do?” section with 4–6 use cases
4. “How VSF works” section
5. Flagship projects / evidence section
6. Clear CTA paths

Suggested headline direction:
- A remarkable plant for soil, water, and community resilience
- Discover how vetiver helps protect land, reduce erosion, and restore possibilities

### About Page
Goal: explain why VSF exists only after the visitor understands the opportunity.

Recommended structure:
- why vetiver matters
- why VSF was created
- what VSF actually does
- values only if tied to action and approach

### Projects Page
Goal: show vetiver in action.

Projects should be framed as examples of what becomes possible with vetiver, not only as internal project records.

### Get Involved Page
Goal: match each kind of visitor with a path.

Paths:
- support field work
- sponsor a project
- propose a project
- partner with VSF
- subscribe for updates

### Stories / Articles
Goal: educate, then route visitors into project-level action.

Every article should answer one practical question about vetiver or one major problem it addresses.

## Tone Guidelines

Use:
- clear
- practical
- generous
- credible
- hopeful
- concrete

Avoid:
- NGO boilerplate
- abstract climate language without a human or land problem attached
- highly technical explanation before the visitor understands the basics
- generic “mission/vision/values” sections with no action or examples

## Reusable Wording Angles

### Explaining vetiver
- Vetiver is a deep-rooted grass used around the world to protect soil and manage water.
- When planted in hedgerows, vetiver helps slow runoff, hold soil in place, and create better conditions for regeneration.
- It is low-cost, resilient, and adaptable to many climates and landscapes.

### Explaining the Vetiver System
- The Vetiver System is a simple but powerful way of planting vetiver in lines that help landscapes keep water, reduce erosion, and stabilize vulnerable ground.
- It is a plant-based approach that can support farmers, communities, and infrastructure at a fraction of the cost of heavy engineering.

### Explaining VSF
- Vetiver Sans Frontières helps communities discover, test, and apply the possibilities of vetiver.
- We support projects through education, technical guidance, partnerships, evidence-building, and public visibility.

## Practical Instructions for Cortex

When rewriting:
1. Assume the reader has never heard of vetiver.
2. Introduce the plant before the organization.
3. Explain benefits through concrete use cases.
4. Keep the first explanation simple; move technical depth lower on the page.
5. Connect every educational section to a project, proof point, or CTA.
6. Reuse the same core language consistently across Home, About, Projects, and Get Involved.
7. Prefer “practical possibilities” over abstract institutional copy.

## Definition of Success

The wording succeeds if a first-time visitor can answer these questions in under a minute:
- What is vetiver?
- Why is it useful?
- What kinds of problems can it solve?
- What does VSF do with it?
- How can I support or join this work?
