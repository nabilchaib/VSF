					<h2>From Fire to Future: Breaking the Cycle of Slash-and-Burn Agriculture</h2>				
		<p data-start="496" data-end="782">In the Congo Basin, millions of smallholder farmers face an impossible choice: feed their families today or preserve the land for tomorrow. Slash-and-burn agriculture remains one of the few accessible methods for clearing fields quickly — but its long-term consequences are devastating.</p><p data-start="784" data-end="926">Soils lose fertility within a few seasons. Farmers are forced to clear new land. Forest cover shrinks. Carbon emissions rise. Poverty deepens.</p><p data-start="928" data-end="994">This cycle is not driven by ignorance — it is driven by necessity.</p><p style="text-align: center;"><strong>The Challenge: A Cycle of Land Degradation and Poverty</strong></p><p>Slash-and-burn agriculture persists because alternatives are often too costly, labor-intensive, or technically complex for rural communities.</p><ul><li><p data-start="1427" data-end="1468">Key impacts observed in affected regions:</p></li><li><p data-start="1427" data-end="1468">Rapid soil fertility loss requiring new land clearing every 2–3 years</p></li><li><p data-start="1427" data-end="1468">Increased erosion and flooding due to exposed soil</p></li><li><p data-start="1427" data-end="1468">Declining crop yields and food insecurity</p></li><li><p data-start="1427" data-end="1468">Loss of forest ecosystems and biodiversity</p></li><li><p data-start="1427" data-end="1468">Significant carbon emissions from burning vegetation</p></li></ul>

		<p style="text-align: center;"><strong><em>"I couldn't believe something so simple could be so powerful," </em></strong></p><p>recalls Eric Mpongo, an agronomist and community innovator. He's spearheading a movement that turns crisis into cultivation. Faced with the deeply ingrained practice of slash-and-burn agriculture, Eric developed a Vetiver corridor system that empowers farmers to shift from destructive cycles to regenerative farming.</p><p data-start="771" data-end="975">With over eight regions now adopting his method Eric’s approach uses 10,000–20,000 Vetiver slips per hectare to mulch crops and naturally restore the soil, reduce labor, and increase yields.</p><p data-start="980" data-end="1205">But Eric didn’t stop there. To educate farmers, he began building a <strong data-start="1109" data-end="1141">10,000-plant Vetiver nursery</strong>, forming a live training hub for agroecological transformation.</p><p data-start="1210" data-end="1377">Working with a major agricultural group, Eric continues to advocate for systemic change, backed not by speeches, but by <strong data-start="1330" data-end="1351">actionable models</strong> other farmers can follow.</p>
